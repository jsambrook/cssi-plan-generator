# TRIZ: Theory of Inventive Problem Solving

Source: Genrich Altshuller, starting 1946. Developed from analysis of hundreds of thousands of patents across many fields. Core insight: inventive problems follow recurring patterns, and the same solution strategies appear across unrelated domains.

---

## Core Philosophy

- **Problems are caused by contradictions.** When improving one thing degrades another, you have a problem worth solving inventively rather than by compromise.
- **Someone, somewhere, has already solved your problem** — just in a different domain. TRIZ abstracts specific problems into general types, matches them to general solution types, then maps back to your specific context.
- **Systems evolve in predictable directions.** You can forecast where a product, process, or business is heading and get there faster.

---

## Two Types of Contradictions

**Technical contradiction:** Improving parameter A degrades parameter B. Example: making a product more thorough makes it slower. This is the same structure as a TOC Evaporating Cloud — two desirable things that appear to conflict.

**Physical contradiction:** A single element must simultaneously have two opposite properties. Example: the service must be both standardized (for efficiency) and customized (for each client). The EC can identify this, but the resolution requires something beyond assumption-challenging — it requires a structural trick.

---

## Ideal Final Result (IFR)

The IFR is a formulation technique that fights psychological inertia — the tendency to think incrementally from the current solution.

**Template**: "The [system] itself performs [desired function], without cost, without harm, without complexity."

**System-level version**: "The ideal system does not exist, but its function is fully performed."

**Examples**:
- "The ideal pipeline generates qualified prospects without any of John's time." (This pushed the energy-catalyst run toward content and referral networks.)
- "The ideal consulting engagement resolves the client's dilemma before the session even starts." (This pushed toward pre-qualification in v005.)
- "The ideal training program trains practitioners without a trainer." (This pushes toward self-service tools + async material.)

**When to use**: The idea is well-developed but feels incremental. It solves a problem but introduces its own complexity. Or the idea has accumulated features across iterations and needs a reset toward radical simplification.

## Four Separation Principles (for Physical Contradictions)

When something must be both X and not-X simultaneously, try each of these:

### 1. Separation in Time
The element has property X at time T1 and property not-X at time T2.

**Business example**: The consulting practice must be both open (to build community) and proprietary (to generate revenue). Separation in time: the methodology is open *before* the engagement (marketing) and the judgment is proprietary *during* the engagement (delivery). (This is essentially what v003 discovered.)

### 2. Separation in Space
Property X in one location, property not-X in another.

**Business example**: The service must be both deep (for complex clients) and accessible (for small ones). Separation in space: Dilemma Lab (accessible, 2.5 hours) for one segment, Mafia Offer Development (deep, 2-4 weeks) for another.

### 3. Separation by Condition
Property X under condition C1, property not-X under condition C2.

**Business example**: The pricing must be both high (to reflect value) and low (to attract first clients). Separation by condition: free for the first Dilemma Lab (when building the case study), premium-priced for all subsequent ones (when the case study exists).

### 4. Separation of Whole and Parts
Parts have property X, the whole has property not-X. (Like a chain: each link is rigid, but the chain is flexible.)

**Business example**: The practice must be both expert-dependent (for quality) and scalable (for growth). Separation of whole and parts: each spoke practitioner has limited expertise (parts are constrained), but the network as a whole has broad coverage (the system is scalable). John's validation ensures quality at the system level.

## Business-Transferable Inventive Principles

Of TRIZ's 40 Inventive Principles, approximately 12 transfer well to business idea improvement. These are solution heuristics drawn from cross-domain patent analysis — they suggest structural changes that analytical moves (clarify, ground, resolve) do not prompt.

| # | Principle | Business Translation | What It Prompts |
|---|-----------|---------------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Segmentation** | Break the offering into independent modules | "Can we sell pieces separately? Can we unbundle?" |
| 2 | **Taking Out** | Remove the part causing the most friction | "What if we removed the hardest component entirely?" |
| 5 | **Merging** | Combine with a complementary product/service | "What adjacent thing should this be bundled with?" |
| 6 | **Universality** | Make one component serve multiple functions | "Can the delivery mechanism also be the marketing mechanism?" |
| 10 | **Preliminary Action** | Pre-perform required changes before they're needed | "Can we do the setup before the client even asks?" |
| 13 | **The Other Way Round** | Reverse the flow | "What if the customer created the thing and we validated it?" |
| 15 | **Dynamics** | Make fixed parameters adjustable | "What if the pricing/scope/feature set adapted per customer?" |
| 22 | **Blessing in Disguise** | Use a harmful factor as a feature | "What if the weakness IS the selling point?" |
| 23 | **Feedback** | Add a feedback loop where there is none | "What if the output improved the next input automatically?" |
| 25 | **Self-Service** | The system maintains/improves itself using its own byproducts | "Can usage data make the system better without extra effort?" |
| 26 | **Copying** | Use a cheap simulation instead of the real thing | "Can we prototype with a simulation instead of building it?" |
| 27 | **Cheap Short-Living** | Replace one expensive durable with many cheap disposable instances | "What if we generated 10 drafts and picked the best, instead of perfecting one?" |

## Trends of Technical Systems Evolution

Altshuller identified 11 patterns that all systems follow as they mature. For business ideas, these predict where the market/technology is heading:

1. **S-curve evolution** — slow start, rapid growth, plateau. Where on the curve is this idea?
2. **Increasing value** — systems evolve to deliver more value per unit of resource.
3. **Transition to supersystem** — systems get absorbed into larger systems. What larger system might absorb this?
4. **Increasing completeness** — systems acquire all necessary subsystems (energy, transmission, control, feedback).
5. **Decreasing human involvement** — automation replaces manual work. (Directly maps to Move 2.)
6. **Increasing trimming** — unnecessary components get eliminated. What can be removed?
7. **Flow enhancement** — better utilization of substance/energy/information flows.
8. **Increasing coordination** — components become better synchronized.
9. **Uneven development** — core function develops first, support catches up later. What support function is lagging?
10. **Increasing controllability** — more ways to control the system.
11. **Increasing dynamization** — static becomes flexible and adaptive.

## What NOT to Import from TRIZ

- **Su-Field Analysis / 76 Standard Solutions** — too engineering-specific for business ideas.
- **Full ARIZ algorithm** (85 steps) — overkill; the valuable pieces (IFR, resource analysis) are captured above.
- **The raw Contradiction Matrix** (39 engineering parameters) — doesn't map to business. Business-adapted matrices exist (Darrell Mann) but add complexity without proportional value for an AI agent that can reason about contradictions directly.
- **Principles 28-40** — deeply physical/chemical (pneumatics, phase transitions, thermal expansion, etc.). Not transferable to business.
