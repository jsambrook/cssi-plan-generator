# Goldratt's Rules for New Technology

Source: Eli Goldratt, *Necessary but Not Sufficient* (2000) and the lecture series *Beyond the Goal*. Also formalized as "Goldratt's Six Questions" in the TOCICO Body of Knowledge.

---

## Core Principle

> "Technology can bring benefits if, and only if, it diminishes a limitation."

Goldratt emphasized the word *can* — diminishing a limitation is necessary but not sufficient. Benefits cannot be obtained unless the old rules built to accommodate the limitation are also changed.

---

## The Four Questions

These are the foundational questions, presented sequentially:

### 1. What is the power of the technology?

Define what the technology *can do* — its inherent capability. This is the only question asked from the perspective of the technology itself. Avoid being impressed without examining further implications.

### 2. What limitation does the technology diminish?

Precisely identify the constraint or barrier the technology eliminates or vastly reduces. Limitations may be invisible — embedded in daily life so thoroughly they are not perceived as limitations. (Example: before automobiles, the limitation that work must be within walking distance of home was not perceived as a limitation because it was universal.)

### 3. What rules helped us accommodate the limitation?

Uncover the behavioral patterns, policies, measurements, and organizational structures that evolved to cope with the old limitation. These rules are often deeply embedded and feel like "the way things are done." They persist long after the limitation they accommodated has been removed.

### 4. What rules should we use now?

Design new rules that exploit the removal of the limitation. Note: the new rules are not necessarily the opposite of the old rules — they require fresh thinking about what is now possible.

---

## The Six Questions (Extended Version)

The TOCICO Body of Knowledge extends Goldratt's framework to six questions for assessing technology value:

1. What is the power of the technology?
2. What limitation does it diminish?
3. What are the old rules that accommodated the limitation?
4. What are the new rules that should replace them?
5. In light of the new rules, what changes are required in the technology?
6. How to cause the change (the resistance to change)?

Questions 5 and 6 address implementation realities: the technology itself may need modification to support the new rules, and organizational change management is required to overcome resistance.

---

## The Formula

New Technology + Changing Old Assumptions (Rules) = Maximum Value

Without changing the rules, new technology merely automates the old way of working — bringing little or no benefit despite the investment.

---

## Application to AI-Augmented TOC

Applying these questions to AI agents that use Thinking Processes:

1. **Power**: AI can generate, critique, and iterate on Evaporating Clouds at speed — dozens of injections in minutes rather than one cloud per workshop.

2. **Limitation diminished**: The cognitive stamina required to build and resolve clouds systematically. Previously, the EC was powerful but rare because it demanded sustained analytical effort from a skilled practitioner.

3. **Old rules**: Clouds are built in workshops with facilitators. One cloud per session. Thinking Processes are reserved for significant, high-stakes decisions. Clients accept dilemmas as real because the cost of resolving them (facilitator time, workshop time) is high.

4. **New rules**: Clouds can be built speculatively and iteratively. Multiple tensions can be surfaced and resolved in a single session. The cost of running a cloud drops to near zero, so every perceived dilemma can be examined. Clients should expect their consultants to resolve tensions, not manage trade-offs.
