## Move rationale
Final iteration. All dimensions 69-78. The tool has accumulated mechanisms across 7 iterations (anti-inflation, drift detection, cross-run learning, front-load-then-run, CLAUDE.md redesign). IFR asks: is this complexity justified? What would the ideal version look like?

## IFR formulation

**"The ideal plan generator does not exist, but its function is fully performed."**

What is the function? Business ideas get refined — they become more concrete, more valuable, more testable, with fewer failure modes, faster.

What would have to be true for this to happen without the tool?
- The person would need to know the 11 moves and apply them instinctively
- They would need to score honestly without a rubric
- They would need to research without being told to
- They would need to catch their own biases

In other words: the ideal plan generator is a *trained practitioner*. The tool exists because practitioners don't have this skill set (TOC + TRIZ + autoresearch + disciplined scoring) integrated and readily available.

**The gap between the tool and the ideal:** The tool adds value by encoding expert methodology into a repeatable process. The IFR doesn't suggest eliminating the tool — it suggests eliminating everything in the tool that isn't the methodology. Every mechanism that is overhead rather than methodology should be scrutinized.

### Changes

1. **Regression check merged into red team step.** The red team step now explicitly includes: "If any score changed by more than 10 points, explain why in the red team section." This eliminates a separate mechanism while preserving the function.

2. **Drift detection triggered by EC injections, not by a timer.** After any Move 5 application, the agent re-reads the seed and checks for drift. No scheduled checks otherwise.

3. **Variant template simplified.** Required sections: Move applied, Move rationale (one sentence), Idea, Red team (including regression check), Scores. Optional: Execution plan, Notes.

## Idea

The cssi-plan-generator is an auto-improving business idea refinement tool that gets better with use. Its design follows the IFR principle: every mechanism is either core methodology or eliminated.

### Core methodology (what makes this tool valuable)
- **11 improvement moves** from TOC and TRIZ
- **5-dimension scoring** with bottleneck comparison
- **Red team step** before scoring (includes regression check for large score changes)
- **Anchored justifications** (every score cites a concrete element)
- **Devil's advocate** required for 85+ scores
- **Cross-run learning** via `results.tsv` scan at run start
- **Front-load-then-run** (interactive seed, autonomous iteration, human review)

### Lightweight safeguards (minimal overhead)
- **Drift detection** triggered after Move 5 (EC) injections only, not on a timer
- **Variant checkpoints** (every variant saved; current-best only updated on wins)
- **Bounded moves** (one move per iteration)
- **Plateau auto-stop** (3 consecutive non-improvements)

### What was removed or simplified
- Regression check → merged into red team step
- Scheduled drift detection → event-triggered (after EC injections only)
- Variant template → simplified to 4 required sections + 2 optional

### CLAUDE.md redesign
- User section: 3 actions (start, iterate, compare)
- Agent section: core methodology + safeguards

### Target status
1. Reduce manual steps — **Done** (62.5% reduction)
2. First-run experience — **Addressed** (CLAUDE.md rewrite)
3. Scoring integrity — **Demonstrated** (v006 rejected; red team + anchored justifications are the primary mechanisms)

## Red team (pre-scoring criticisms)

1. Merging regression check into red team risks losing it in practice. The red team step is already doing several things (criticisms, large score changes). Adding more responsibilities to one step may dilute its focus. Counter: the red team step is the moment of honest self-assessment — regression checking is naturally part of that moment.
2. Changing drift detection from scheduled to event-triggered assumes that Move 5 is the only source of drift. Scope creep from multiple small iterations (Moves 1-4) could also cause drift. Counter: one-move-at-a-time + bottleneck comparison structurally limits creep. The bigger risk is always a single EC injection that reframes the idea.
3. The IFR analysis is subjective — I classified mechanisms as "methodology" or "overhead" based on my own judgment. A different agent might classify differently. Counter: the IFR's value is in forcing the question, not in the specific answers. Even if the classifications were wrong, the exercise trimmed the idea.

**Regression check (within red team):** No score changed by more than 10 points from current-best (v007). Largest moves: Failure modes 69 → 71 (+2), Value 78 → 78 (unchanged).

## Notes

The IFR revealed that the tool had accumulated protective mechanisms faster than it had validated them. Three mechanisms (red team, anchored justifications, bottleneck rule) do most of the work. The others were reasonable additions during iteration but didn't survive the "methodology or overhead?" test.

This is a fitting final iteration: the tool's own improvement process, applied to itself, converged on simplicity. The autoresearch pattern works — but the strongest output is often the one with the fewest moving parts.
